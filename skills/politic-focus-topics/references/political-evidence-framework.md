# Political Evidence Framework

Use this reference when the request needs a more rigorous political-attention workflow, a scoring rubric, or a reusable output template.

## Source Priority

Use the strongest source that fits the claim.

- Executive action and agency policy: prefer official government sites, executive orders, agency releases, regulatory text, and implementation memos.
- Legislative activity: prefer official bill text, committee pages, hearing schedules, vote tallies, whip notices when public, and leadership statements.
- Courts and legal developments: prefer court opinions, orders, dockets, and official filings before commentary.
- Elections and campaigns: prefer election-authority sites, campaign-finance filings, official campaign statements, debate transcripts, and transparent polling sources.
- Diplomacy and security: prefer foreign ministries, treaty organizations, summit communiques, sanctions announcements, defense departments, and official readouts.
- Narrative confirmation: use high-quality reporting to see whether a topic is recurring across coverage rather than appearing as a one-off headline.

## Theme Scoring

Score each candidate theme from 0 to 3 in each category.

- Coverage breadth: how many independent, recent sources mention the same theme.
- Primary-source confirmation: how directly the claim is supported by official materials or first-hand documentation.
- Institutional significance: how much the theme could affect policy, elections, governing coalitions, legal constraints, or geopolitics.
- Catalyst proximity: whether a vote, ruling, filing deadline, debate, summit, enforcement action, or other decision point is imminent or still unfolding.

Interpret the total score this way:

- 9 to 12: dominant political focus.
- 6 to 8: active but secondary theme.
- 3 to 5: watchlist item or emerging narrative.
- 0 to 2: weak evidence or noise.

## Synthesis Checklist

Before finalizing the answer, confirm all of the following:

- The top themes are supported by more than one recent signal, or by one authoritative official action with clear downstream impact.
- The dates in the response are explicit.
- Each theme has a clear why-now explanation.
- The answer separates proposal, passage, implementation, litigation, and enforcement.
- The affected actors, institutions, or geographies are named directly.
- Upcoming catalysts are named when they matter.

## Suggested Response Template

Use or adapt this structure:

1. `Focus Now`
Name the top 3 to 5 political themes in rank order.

2. `Theme Breakdown`
For each theme, explain the catalyst, affected actors or institutions, policy or electoral transmission path, and evidence.

3. `Institutional Read`
Summarize what the executive branch, legislature, courts, campaigns, and international actors are signaling together or where they disagree.

4. `What To Watch Next`
List the next votes, hearings, rulings, filing deadlines, debates, summits, speeches, or implementation milestones that could rotate attention.

5. `Confidence And Gaps`
State where the evidence is strong, mixed, or incomplete.
