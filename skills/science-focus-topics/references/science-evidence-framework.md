# Science Evidence Framework

Use this reference when the request needs a more rigorous science-attention workflow, a scoring rubric, or a reusable output template.

## Source Priority

Use the strongest source that fits the claim.

- Research publications: prefer peer-reviewed journal articles, accepted manuscripts, supplementary materials, and author-provided datasets or code.
- Early-stage findings: prefer preprint servers, conference proceedings, posters, and abstracts, but clearly label the evidence stage and limitations.
- Clinical and health developments: prefer trial registries, regulator notices, public-health agencies, study protocols, and published results before commentary.
- Space, climate, and physical science updates: prefer agency releases, mission dashboards, observatory updates, official instrument teams, and published papers.
- Institutional and funding context: prefer official grantmakers, academies, universities, and lab announcements for what was actually awarded, launched, or stated.
- Narrative confirmation: use high-quality reporting to see whether a topic is recurring across coverage rather than appearing as a one-off headline.

## Theme Scoring

Score each candidate theme from 0 to 3 in each category.

- Coverage breadth: how many independent, recent sources mention the same theme.
- Primary-source confirmation: how directly the claim is supported by papers, datasets, registries, or official scientific institutions.
- Scientific significance: how much the theme could affect research agendas, public understanding, health, policy, or commercial development.
- Catalyst proximity: whether a publication, conference, mission event, trial readout, funding decision, or replication milestone is imminent or still unfolding.

Interpret the total score this way:

- 9 to 12: dominant science focus.
- 6 to 8: active but secondary theme.
- 3 to 5: watchlist item or emerging narrative.
- 0 to 2: weak evidence or noise.

## Synthesis Checklist

Before finalizing the answer, confirm all of the following:

- The top themes are supported by more than one recent signal, or by one authoritative paper, mission event, or agency action with clear downstream impact.
- The dates in the response are explicit.
- Each theme has a clear why-now explanation.
- The answer separates preprint, peer review, replication, and application.
- The affected disciplines, institutions, or populations are named directly.
- Upcoming catalysts are named when they matter.

## Suggested Response Template

Use or adapt this structure:

1. `Focus Now`
Name the top 3 to 5 science themes in rank order.

2. `Theme Breakdown`
For each theme, explain the catalyst, affected fields or institutions, research or policy transmission path, and evidence.

3. `Research Read`
Summarize what journals, agencies, labs, and the broader scientific community are signaling together or where they disagree.

4. `What To Watch Next`
List the next journal releases, conferences, trial readouts, mission milestones, agency decisions, or replication checkpoints that could rotate attention.

5. `Confidence And Gaps`
State where the evidence is strong, mixed, or incomplete.
